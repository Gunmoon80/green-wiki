2. Syllable Structure and Phonotactics

  • Preferred syllable structure is (C)V(C), where C = consonant, V = vowel or diphthong.
  • Clusters of consonants are resolved by inserting epenthetic vowels ("a", "e", or "ə") as needed.
  • Words ending in a consonant are suffixed with a schwa-like "e" ([ə]).
  • Onset clusters are permitted only if both consonants are sonorants (l, r, m, n, w, y).
  • Coda clusters are not permitted; break them with epenthesis.
  • Loanwords are adapted to fit these rules.

Example: dbu ("cat" shifted) is pronounced [daˈbu] or [daːˈbuː].

3. Morphophonemic and Sandhi Rules

  • Assimilation: Adjacent consonants may assimilate in voicing (e.g., "db" becomes [db] or [bb] if between vowels).
  • Vowel Reduction: Unstressed vowels in medial syllables may reduce to [ə].
  • Sandhi: Across word boundaries, final vowels may merge with initial vowels of the next word (e.g., "e a" → [jaː]).
  • Nasalization: Any vowel before "m" or "n" may be nasalized, especially in rapid or informal speech.
  • Elision: In fast speech, unstressed initial vowels may be dropped if the previous word ends in a vowel.

4. Prosody, Stress, and Intonation

  • Primary stress is always on the first syllable. Secondary stress falls on alternating syllables.
  • Intonation patterns:
    • Statements: Level or slightly falling intonation.
    • Questions: Rising intonation on the final syllable.
    • Commands: High, sharp initial pitch, falling at the end.
    • Emphasis: Lengthen and raise pitch of the stressed vowel.
  • Pitch accent: For poetic or formal speech, a high pitch may mark contrastive focus.
  • Rhythm: Syllable-timed; all syllables are pronounced with roughly equal duration, except for lengthened vowels under stress.

5. Register and Sociolinguistics

  • Formal register: Use full vowels, avoid elision, and maintain strict syllable structure.
  • Informal register: Allow more elision, assimilation, and use of nasal vowels. Diphthongs and expressive intonation are more common.
  • Poetic register: Employ vowel harmony, pitch accent, and extended vowel length for artistic effect.

6. Grammatical Conventions

  • No inflection for tense, number, or case. All grammatical information is carried by word order and context.
  • Questions are indicated solely by a rising intonation at the end of the sentence.
  • Proper nouns retain capitalization but follow the same shifting and pronunciation rules.
  • Negation is expressed by the particle no (shifted: op), placed before the verb.
  • Emphasis is marked by lengthening the stressed vowel or repeating the word.

7. Orthographic and Punctuation Notes

  • All punctuation is preserved from the source language.
  • Numbers are not shifted and are pronounced as in English.
  • Non-alphabetic characters (hyphens, apostrophes) are pronounced as brief pauses.
  • Capitalization is retained for proper nouns and sentence-initial words.

9. Advanced Usage and Style

  • Alliteration and Assonance: In poetry or song, maximize repetition of consonant or vowel sounds for effect.
  • Reduplication: Repeat a word or syllable for emphasis or plurality (e.g., dbu-dbu for "many cats").
  • Expressive Lengthening: Vowels may be lengthened up to three times for dramatic effect in exclamations.
  • Code-switching: In multilingual contexts, English words may be inserted without shifting, but must be pronounced with purplish phonology.

11. Summary of Procedure

  1. Shift each letter of the source word forward by one in the English alphabet (A→B, ..., Z→A).
  2. Segment the shifted word into pronounceable syllables, inserting vowels as required.
  3. Apply all phonological, morphophonemic, and prosodic rules described above.
  4. Maintain primary stress on the first syllable of each word.
  5. Observe neutral, rising, or emphatic intonation as appropriate.

12. Appendix: Special Cases

  • If the shifted form results in ambiguous pronunciation, default to the closest CV or VC syllable structure.
  • For loanwords or technical terms, shift as normal and adapt to the syllable and pronunciation rules.
  • For acronyms, shift each letter and pronounce as a sequence of syllables.
  • For poetic or dramatic speech, all rules may be bent for artistic effect, provided mutual intelligibility is maintained.
